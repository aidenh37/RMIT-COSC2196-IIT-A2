Employer demand

The career paths which the group have chosen are all in-demand positions, as per data sourced from Burning Glass Technologies. This has allowed us to collate and analyse the demand for these positions from employers.

  • Aiden/Helen: Software Developer/Engineer - 29456 job opportunities - strong demand
  • Adam/Joshua: Web Developer - 5990 job opportunities - strong demand
  • Sean: Developer/Programmer - 4436 job opportunities - strong demand
  • Robert: Business Development Manager - 4141 job opportunities - strong demand

Required skill sets

General skills that are consistent across the career paths include:

  • Soft skills – these are personal attributes, for example; emotional intelligence, team player attitude, growth mindset, openness to feedback, adaptability, work ethic and active listening
  • Teamwork – the ability to work collaboratively with others
  • Problem-solving – the ability to think outside the box to solve a problem or equally seek guidance from colleagues as needed
  • Relationship management – the ability to effectively manage and enjoy good relations with managers, colleagues and external stakeholders
  • Troubleshooting – being able to walk through a problem step-by-step until an adequate solution can be reached

IT-specific skills that are consistent across the career paths include:

  • Systems and networks – these are often specific to the position although they can also be quite generic – for example, CRM
  • Multiple back-end programming languages – for example; Python, Java, C# /li>
  • Multiple front-end programming languages – for example; HTML/CSS, JavaScript, XML
  • Machine learning – a type of artificial intelligence (AI) that allows software applications to become more accurate at predicting outcomes for example; Music reading software
  • Security – the ability to maintain, upgrade and implement security measures
  • DevOps – the ability to maintain a testing environment
  • Data analysis – the ability to comprehend and analyse data based on company specifications

IT-specific skills demand and ranking

Strong Coding Skills occur in 80% of employer-demanded skills for our group’s desired jobs. Occurring 30% of the time is experience with GitHub as well as databases. With far less demand at only 17% is the requirement for experience with Microsoft Office, software architecture and Windows and/or Windows Server. From this data, it is clear that employers favour strong programming skills in new employees.

General skills demand and ranking

Problem-solving, as well as written and verbal communication, occur in 100% of employer-demanded skills for our group’s desired jobs. 80% of the time the ability to work in a team is a required skill. With less demand – required 30% of the time – are interpersonal, time and project management skills. 20% of the time negotiation skills and experience with Microsoft Office are essential. From this data, it is clear that employers favour problem solving, and good written and verbal communication skills among new employees.

IT-specific skills not in the group skill set

Cybersecurity, helpdesk and AI engineering experience are three of the highest-ranked IT-specific skills not in our required skill set. For example, cybersecurity experience is most in demand from jobs such as CISO, Cybersecurity Engineer or Penetration Tester, as these positions require knowledge of maintaining information security and risk management as well as strong knowledge in networking, and encryption techniques. Whereas AI engineering is more in demand for positions such as Data Scientists and Machine Learning Engineer where the position requires optimising customer experiences



General skills not in the group skill set

Creativity, organisation, and perseverance personality traits are three of the highest-ranked general requirements not in our skill set. For example, organisation and perseverance qualities are most in demand from positions such as helpdesk support. Whereas creativity is essential in positions such as Graphic Designer.

Helen’s opinion after a review of the data

Reviewing the data from Burning Glass has not changed my mind regarding the career path I have chosen. Analysing the data has allowed me to conclude that I have chosen the appropriate career choice for my skill set and has allowed me to bring attention to areas of soft skills which would require my focus.
